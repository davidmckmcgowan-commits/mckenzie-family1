The journey

Your family crossed the world over 75 years. Here's how they got here.

~1767
Invergordon, Scottish Highlands
🏴󠁧󠁢󠁳󠁣󠁴󠁿 Scotland
Hugh McKenzie Sr is born in the rugged Highlands of northern Scotland. The area is beautiful but harsh — people farm, fish, and trade along the coast. The McKenzies are part of the proud McKenzie clan, whose ancient gathering place was a hill called Tulloch.
1794 – 1802
Ireland — fighting in a rebellion
🇮🇪 Ireland
Hugh joins the Caithness Legion and is sent to Ireland, where a violent uprising against British rule is taking place. He fights and survives. He serves over 7 years, reaching the rank of Sergeant. Near the town of Drogheda he meets Bridget Erskine, and they marry around 1801.
"He served 7 years and 7 months, finishing as a sergeant." — family records
1803 – 1825
Kirkcudbright & Balmaghie, Scotland
🏴󠁧󠁢󠁳󠁣󠁴󠁿 Scotland
The family returns to Scotland — but to the south-west, far from where Hugh grew up. Children are born: John (1803), Hugh Jr (1804), Kenneth (1806), and others. Hugh probably works in coastal trading and possibly whaling. Life is reasonable, but things are about to change.
1826
New Brunswick, Canada
🇨🇦 Canada
The family is forced off their land in the Highland Clearances — rich landlords evicted thousands of Scottish tenant families to make room for sheep farming. One source calls Hugh "an exile from Scotland." The McKenzies sail to Canada and arrive in St John, New Brunswick. Within 10 days of arriving, Hugh applies for 200 acres of land!
The Highland Clearances were one of the most painful events in Scottish history — whole communities were destroyed overnight.
1826 – 1840
Nova Scotia & New Brunswick, Canada
🇨🇦 Canada
The family builds a life in Canada. More children are born, including John — the youngest son — in April 1840 in Nova Scotia, just months before the next great journey. Hugh Jr becomes a ship's captain, commanding vessels across the Atlantic. He sails coal and cargo from Ireland to England, and travels as far as Jamaica.
October 1840
Setting sail for Australia
⛵ At sea
The extended McKenzie family boards a ship called "The Brothers" and sets sail for the other side of the world. On board is 6-month-old baby John, who would go on to become one of Albany's greatest citizens. The voyage takes about 2 months.
December 1840
Albany, Western Australia
🇦🇺 Australia
The family arrives in Albany — a tiny settlement of around 200 people on the south coast of Western Australia. It's remote, rugged, and full of opportunity. Tragically, Hugh Sr dies just weeks later in January 1841, aged about 74. But the family stays, and Albany becomes the McKenzie home for generations.
"When 'The Brothers' arrived in Albany in December 1840, on board was eight-month old Canadian born John." — family history
1840 onwards
Building Albany
🇦🇺 Albany, WA
The McKenzies don't just live in Albany — they help build it. Hugh Jr runs the Ship Inn. John becomes a whaler at 14, then buys the Freemasons Hotel, becomes Mayor three times, fights for public rights to the foreshore, establishes Albany's first Masonic lodge, and is remembered as one of the town's founding fathers. His brother Cuthbert becomes a Member of Parliament.

Cool stories

Your ancestors had remarkable lives. Here are the most extraordinary moments.

⚔️
The soldier who crossed the world
Hugh Sr fought in a real rebellion — then somehow ended up in Western Australia.

Hugh McKenzie Sr joined a military unit called the Caithness Legion and was sent to Ireland during the Irish Rebellion of 1798 — a violent uprising against British rule. He fought, survived, and served for over 7 years, reaching the rank of Sergeant.

After leaving the army, he returned to Scotland, had a family, and then got thrown off his land in the Highland Clearances. He moved his family to Canada, built a new life, and then — in his 70s — got on yet another ship to Australia.

He arrived in Albany in December 1840 and died just weeks later in January 1841, aged about 74. He had lived in three countries on three continents and crossed the Atlantic twice.

"An exile from Scotland" — how one source described Hugh Sr after the Highland Clearances
🚢
Ship captain Hugh Jr
Your ancestor commanded real ships across oceans — and got shot at in his own hotel.

Hugh McKenzie Jr became a professional ship's captain. He commanded the "Rob Roy" in 1833, sailing from Ireland to Liverpool with coal and salt. In 1834 he captained the "Trial" at Kingston, Jamaica. In 1836 he mastered the "Fenwick Keating."

After arriving in Albany, he leased the Ship Inn — a pub right on the waterfront where whalers would drink. He had a white cockatoo above the bar that had been taught to say rude things to customers.

"Hello! Drunk again? Get out, you black cow!" — the cockatoo's favourite greeting

One day a French whaling captain wasn't impressed and took a shot at Hugh in his own hotel. Hugh survived!

🐋
John the teenage whaler
At just 14, John went out whale hunting to support his widowed mother.

When John's father Hugh Jr died in 1854, his mother was widowed with several young children still at home. John was just 14 years old. He left school and went to work on whaling ships at Cheyne Beach Station, 40 miles east of Albany.

Whaling was incredibly dangerous and exhausting. Once a whale was spotted, the crew rowed out in small boats to harpoon it. A wounded whale could drag the boat at speed for hours, or turn and attack. After the whale tired, it was hauled back to the station and its blubber boiled down into oil — used for lamps, medicine and cosmetics.

John worked in whaling for 22 years. In 1876, a whale attacked his boat, killing two of his crew and fracturing several of John's ribs. He had to stop. He was 36 years old.

"He had a bad mishap while whaling at Doubtful Island Bay. Two men in his boat were killed by a small whale which attacked the boat. John was knocked overboard but scrambled back on." — Captain Sale's memoirs

Even decades later, in 1908, when a whale was found stranded near Albany, 68-year-old John rushed down with a harpoon "with all the enthusiasm of a veteran whaler" and killed it himself — using a harpoon that hadn't been used in 41 years!

🏨
The hotel dynasty
Three generations of McKenzies ran pubs in early Albany.

The McKenzies were central to Albany's social life for decades — through their hotels. Hugh Jr leased the Ship Inn in the 1840s, right on the waterfront. His son John bought the Freemasons Hotel in 1870 for £1,000.

John ran the Freemasons for years, then leased it out. In 1904 he spent nearly £3,000 on a massive renovation — turning it into a three-storey hotel described as being in the "Italian Renaissance style" and resembling "Monte Carlo." It had 56-60 rooms and was called "a model of its kind."

"In raising this fine building he has manifested a public spirit and a belief in the future of Albany which are exactly the qualities that make for the accomplishment of what is desirable." — Albany newspaper, 1905

John's son William later ran the Balara Hotel in Narrogin, and John also owned the Pier Hotel in Esperance and the Federal Hotel in Wagin. Hotels were the McKenzie business for over 60 years.

🪓
John chops down the fence
When a company fenced off Albany's foreshore, John grabbed an axe.

In the late 1880s, a company called the Western Australian Land Company built a railway line to Albany. The problem? They wanted to run it along the waterfront — blocking the townspeople's access to their own harbour.

The company started erecting fences on public streets. Albany residents were furious. John McKenzie — then in his late 40s and one of the town's most respected figures — decided to take direct action.

"John McKenzie personally went to the foot of Bridges Street with an axe, and chopped down a fence which had been illegally erected by the W.A. Land Company, thus starting a 'row' which terminated with the people of Albany asserting their right to be masters in their own town." — Albany memoir

The town council took the company to the Supreme Court — and won. The streets and access to the foreshore remained open to the public. John was a key witness in the case.

⚗️
Ernest the goldfields engineer
From railway workshops to the gold rush — Ernest had the most varied career.

Ernest George McKenzie, John's youngest son, was born in 1877. In 1895, aged 18, he started working at the Albany Railway workshops. In 1897 he headed to the goldfields at Norseman. He then worked on ships for the Adelaide Steamship Company.

In 1901, he graduated from the School of Mines in Adelaide, passing exams in assaying, chemistry, machine design, fitting and turning. He became a qualified mining engineer and went to work managing State gold batteries on the goldfields — including at Ora Banda, a remote mining town in the WA outback.

While studying in Adelaide he met Isabelle Niven Pearson, whose mother and aunt ran a school for girls. Isabelle was an accomplished musician and artist. They married in Albany in 1904.

In 1906, their daughter Margaret was born in Perth. In 1909, a son named John — after his grandfather — was born. Ernest died in 1941 and is buried at Karrakatta Cemetery.

🏛️
John: Mayor, JP, and Freemason
After hanging up his harpoon, John became the most important person in Albany.

After his whaling accident in 1876, John retired from the sea and threw himself into building Albany. The list of things he did is remarkable:

  • Bought the Freemasons Hotel for £1,000 in 1870
  • Founded Albany's first Masonic Lodge in 1873 — the first lodge to build a hall in Western Australia
  • Elected to Albany Council in 1888; served 17 years
  • Elected Mayor three times: 1891, 1892, 1893
  • Appointed Justice of the Peace in 1894
  • Founded the King George Sound Coal Company in 1883
  • Helped found the Albany Turf Club
  • Donated land for the Albany Tennis Club
  • Won first prize for his potatoes at the 1902 Albany Show!

When he died in 1914, his estate was valued at £11,526 — worth several million dollars today. He owned 17 parcels of land across WA.

"Although not a native of the State, Mr. McKenzie was brought here as a young baby and he spent almost the full 74 years of his life in the town." — eulogy, 1914
🌋
Johnny sails past Krakatoa
John's son was at sea when the most destructive volcanic eruption in history happened.

John Edward McKenzie Jr (known as Johnny) left Albany in 1883 at age 17 with his brother-in-law James Clark. They had bought a small vessel called the Ashburton and sailed north to establish a trading business at the mouth of the Ashburton River in the remote north-west of WA (now the town of Onslow).

The voyage took 11 days. As they arrived at the Ashburton River...

"The great Java Straits earthquake took place, causing a tidal wave to rush up the river and resulting in a great commotion." — family history

This was the eruption of Krakatoa — one of the most violent volcanic events ever recorded. The explosion was heard 5,000 kilometres away and killed over 36,000 people. Johnny and James would have felt the resulting tidal wave surge up the river around their boat.

Despite this dramatic beginning, their business — Clarke & McKenzie — became very successful, operating as traders, shippers, and eventually pearlers. Sadly, Johnny died in Onslow in 1897, aged just 31, from a liver condition caused by the harsh climate.

Family mysteries

Every family has unanswered questions. Here are the things your family is still trying to figure out. Maybe you can help solve them one day!

Unsolved mysteries 🔍

When was Hugh Sr actually born?
His tombstone suggests 1766, but land grant documents say 1769. Even the family historians aren't sure. It was probably somewhere between 1767 and 1769.
Who did Hugh Laurie (Gen 4) marry?
John's letter from 1882 says "Hugh is married" but no marriage record has ever been found. His wife may have died before him. He's buried with his uncle Hugh and cousin James — not with a wife.
What happened to Emily (Gen 3)?
Emily was Hugh Jr's daughter, born around 1832. She simply disappears from records. She doesn't appear in WA records at all. Did she move interstate? Nobody knows.
Was Isabelle Hind from Scotland or England?
Hugh Jr's wife Isabelle (your great-great-grandmother) is described in one source as being born in "Preston, Lancashire" — in England. But the family came from Scotland. We don't yet know where she was really from or how she met Hugh.
Was Leslie actually Sabina's child?
Albert's daughter Leslie Evelyn (born 1905) has duplicate birth certificates — one naming Albert's wife Catherine as the mother, and one naming Albert's sister Sabina. Family researcher Peter Brown suspects Sabina was the real mother. But nobody knows for sure.
Was there a brother called James in Gen 3?
Some records suggest Hugh Jr had a son called James who is buried in Albany. But the family historian believes there was no James, because the Scottish naming tradition was strictly followed and the first son would have been named Hugh — which he was.
